\section{Introduction}

This paper asks whether policy credit responds to and mitigates upstream financial pressure created by downstream input-demand wedges in domestic production networks. The current first-stage framing is deliberately cautious: the project measures an industry-level domestic intermediate input-demand wedge and tests whether it has a buyer-power interpretation, rather than assuming that the wedge is a pure markdown.

The core mechanism has four steps. First, some downstream industries may face or exercise wedges in their demand for intermediate inputs. Second, upstream industries that sell disproportionately to these downstream industries inherit greater exposure through input-output linkages. Third, exposed upstream firms may face weaker cash flow, longer payment cycles, higher financing pressure, and lower investment. Fourth, preferential credit or supply-chain-oriented financial support may either mitigate these pressures or preserve payment dependence and weak firms.

The empirical contribution is to separate a Wang-style own-markup credit allocation mechanism from a production-network exposure mechanism. Own markup remains a benchmark and control, while the new object is upstream exposure to downstream input-demand wedges.
